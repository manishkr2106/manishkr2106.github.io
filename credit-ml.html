01 — The Problem

Credit Risk Is a
Sensitivity Problem

Every loan approval is a prediction. The "Give Me Some Credit" Kaggle dataset gives us 150,000 real borrower records with a binary target: did they experience serious financial distress (90+ days delinquent) within the next two years? The business stakes are asymmetric — a false negative (approving a future defaulter) means real cash lost; a false positive (rejecting a good borrower) means lost revenue but no direct loss. This drives every modeling decision: sensitivity is the metric that maps to dollars.

The hardest problem: only 6.68% of borrowers defaulted — a 14:1 class imbalance. A model predicting everyone as safe scores 93.25% accuracy while catching 0% of defaulters. Accuracy is worse than useless here — it actively misleads. AUC-ROC and Sensitivity are the real KPIs.

02 — Data Cleaning (Phase 2)

10-Step Cleaning
Pipeline in R

Phase 2 replaced the minimal Phase 1 cleaning with a comprehensive 10-step pipeline — addressing every problematic column with a documented strategy, trade-off, and verification check. Three imputation methods were tested (mean, sample/bootstrap, CART via mice) and found to produce virtually identical model performance (AUC range: 0.85–0.86, sensitivity difference <1%). Mean imputation was selected for simplicity and reproducibility.

Impact of Cleaning + Class Weighting on LR

Phase 1 LR (minimal cleaning, no class weights): Sensitivity = 4%, AUC = 0.69. Phase 2 LR (full cleaning + 1:14 weights): Sensitivity = 74.4%, AUC = 0.85. The dominant factor was class weighting — it fundamentally changed what the model optimized for. Comprehensive data cleaning contributed incremental improvements to overall discrimination quality on top of that foundation.

The Neural Network was implemented with 13 input nodes → 4 hidden nodes → 1 output node — 61 total learned weight parameters. All 13 standardized variables serve as inputs. The 4 hidden nodes detect combinations of risk factors that no single variable captures alone. H2 and H3 carry the strongest weights into the output node. The strongest input signals come from NumberOfTimes90DaysLate, RevolvingUtilization, and NumberOfTime60-89DaysPastDue — consistent with CART's variable importance ranking.

Why does NN outperform LR despite using the same inputs and class weights? Non-linear interaction detection. A borrower with both high credit utilization and serious prior delinquency carries dramatically higher risk than either factor alone would suggest linearly. The NN's hidden layer learns these multiplicative risk combinations automatically — LR requires manual engineering of interaction terms to achieve the same effect.

04 — Threshold Analysis

Cutoff Calibration —
Sensitivity vs. Specificity Tradeoff

All six models were evaluated at a 0.5 cutoff for consistent comparison. But in a real bank deployment, the right threshold depends on the institution's cost structure — the ratio of financial loss from a bad loan vs. business cost of turning away a good customer. We tested three thresholds using LR with mean imputation:

CutoffSensitivitySpecificityInterpretation
0.5 (standard)74.4%80.0%Catches 3 in 4 defaulters; clears 4 in 5 safe borrowers
0.482.7%71.8%Catches more defaulters; more false alarms on safe borrowers
0.388.4%61.6%Catches 9 in 10 defaulters; flags ~4 in 10 safe borrowers incorrectly

If the financial loss of one missed default equals the business cost of turning away 5 good customers, a cutoff of 0.4 is optimal. If the ratio is 10:1, 0.3 becomes appropriate. This is the most important practical calibration step before any real deployment — and it requires business input, not just statistical optimization.

05 — Ensemble Learning: Stacking

Three Meta-Models —
Does Stacking Add Value?

Stacking combines multiple individual models by training a meta-model on their outputs. Different base models make different kinds of errors — a meta-model learns the optimal way to combine their predictions. We implemented stacking with all three meta-model options: Neural Network, Logistic Regression, and CART — each trained with 1:14 class weights on a stacked dataset of 17 variables (13 original + 3 base model predictions).

Step 1 — Train Base Models
Train LR, CART, and NN on 90,000 training records with full cleaning, standardization, and 1:14 class weights.

Step 2 — Generate Full-Dataset Predictions
Apply all three base models to the full 150,000-record dataset to produce predicted default probabilities. Probability ranges confirmed: LR [0.024, 1.0] · CART [0, 1.0] · NN [0.011, 0.968] — all well-calibrated.
↓
Step 3 — Build Stacked Dataset
Append three new probability columns (pred_LR, pred_CART, pred_NN) to the original dataset → 17 total variables, 150,000 rows confirmed via dim(df_stacked).
↓
Step 4 — Train Three Meta-Models
Train NN meta-model (size=6, maxit=200), LR meta-model (glm(family=binomial)), and CART meta-model (conservative stopping rules + loss matrix) — each on the 90,000 stacked training rows with 1:14 class weights.

A revealing finding from the CART meta-model's variable importance: the three most important inputs were pred_NN, pred_LR, and pred_CART — scoring far higher than any original borrower variable. The meta-model is not re-learning from scratch; it is deciding how much to trust each base model. The Neural Network scored highest — confirming it provides the strongest signal of the three.

Does Stacking Add Value? — Honest Answer: Marginally

All three stacked models perform within ~1 percentage point of the base Neural Network on every metric. The LR meta-model ties the highest AUC (0.86) but has lower sensitivity (76.9%) than the base NN (77.8%) — the metric that matters most in credit risk. Stacking adds significant pipeline complexity (17-variable stacked dataset, 4 model training runs, meta-model selection) for essentially no practical gain on this dataset. The base models are already doing a good job, and the meta-model adds little the individual models haven't already figured out together.

07 — Key Findings

What the Pipeline
Revealed

1. Class weighting is the most impactful single decision. Changing from no weights to 1:14 weighting transformed LR sensitivity from ~4% to 74.4% — a 19× improvement — with virtually no change in the underlying data or model architecture. In imbalanced classification, how you weight the loss function matters more than which algorithm you choose.

2. Past payment behavior dominates all models. NumberOfTimes90DaysLate is the single strongest predictor across all six models — scoring 1,389 in CART importance (3× the next variable), largest standardized coefficient in LR, and strongest input node in the NN. This is consistent with how FICO scores are constructed — payment history accounts for 35% of the score.

3. Neural Networks outperform LR by detecting non-linear interactions. Same inputs, same class weights, same data — but NN achieves 77.8% sensitivity vs. LR's 74.4% (and 0.86 vs. 0.85 AUC) because hidden nodes learn multiplicative risk combinations that a linear model requires manual feature engineering to capture.

4. Stacking adds complexity without meaningful return here. All stacked models perform within 1 percentage point of the base NN. When base models are already well-tuned and diverse, the meta-model has little residual error to correct. Stacking would add more value with weaker or more diverse base models.

5. Imputation method doesn't matter much. Mean, bootstrap, and CART imputation via mice produced AUC differences of less than 0.01 and sensitivity differences of less than 1 percentage point. For datasets where 80% of data is complete, imputation method is a second-order concern.

Recommendation — Neural Network, Calibrate the Threshold

Deploy the Neural Network (77.8% sensitivity, 0.86 AUC) as the primary scoring model. Before deployment, calibrate the probability cutoff below 0.5 based on the bank's actual cost structure — at 0.3, sensitivity reaches ~88% at the cost of lower specificity. The bank's credit officers should determine the threshold by computing: loss from one bad loan ÷ revenue forgone per rejected good customer. That ratio is the optimal cutoff on the ROC curve.

08 — Future Directions

What Would
Come Next

SMOTE (Synthetic Minority Oversampling): Generate synthetic defaulter records to balance the training set before fitting — especially beneficial for CART, which responds poorly to class weights but well to balanced training data. Random Forest & Gradient Boosting: Ensemble tree methods that combine hundreds of decision trees — typically outperform both single CART and neural networks on imbalanced financial datasets. These are the natural next models to benchmark. Feature engineering: Interaction terms (utilization × delinquency count), log-transformed ratio features, and time-since-last-delinquency if available. Production threshold calibration: The most important practical step — run a cost-benefit analysis on the bank's actual loan performance data to determine the exact cutoff that maximizes expected profit under the model's error distribution.
