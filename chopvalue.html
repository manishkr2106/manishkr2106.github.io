01 — What Is It

Go-to-Market Strategy
for a Circular Economy Startup

A full consulting engagement for ChopValue Boston — a certified B Corp that collects used chopsticks from Asian restaurants and upcycles them into premium bamboo furniture and surfaces. The project delivered pricing strategy, price elasticity analysis, customer segmentation, and a 30-slide executive presentation through Babson's Consulting Experience (BCE) program — a live client engagement, not a classroom simulation.

ChopValue's model is a real circular economy operation: collect disposable chopsticks (over 80 billion discarded globally per year), compress and treat them into structural bamboo material, and manufacture furniture. The Boston franchise needed to answer a fundamental business question: how do you price a premium sustainable product in a cost-conscious market without undermining the brand?

02 — Why We Did It

The Problem:
Price Without Undermining Mission

ChopValue sits at a genuine strategic tension: their products cost more to produce than conventional furniture because sustainability is built into the supply chain. But their target customers include both mission-aligned buyers who will pay a premium and cost-sensitive B2B buyers (restaurants, co-working spaces) who evaluate price per unit above all else.

Price too high, and you serve only the luxury segment — missing the mass market and the mission. Price too low, and you signal commodity status, compress margins, and undermine the circular economy positioning that justifies the premium. Finding the price that maximizes both revenue and brand equity simultaneously requires rigorous analysis, not intuition.

"The circular economy story is the product's biggest differentiator — and it was being undersold. Every chopstick has a journey. That journey is the marketing."

03 — How We Did It

Five-Phase
Consulting Process

01
Stakeholder Discovery
Conducted structured interviews with ChopValue Boston leadership, B2B clients (restaurants, co-working spaces), and target B2C consumers. Mapped the job-to-be-done for each segment — functional, emotional, and social.
02
Competitive Landscape Mapping
Benchmarked ChopValue against premium sustainable furniture brands (Burrow, Floyd, Maiden Home) and mass-market alternatives. Identified the white space: B Corp ethics at design-forward aesthetics price points — a segment with almost no competition.
03
Price Elasticity Analysis
Used conjoint-style survey methodology to measure willingness-to-pay across B2B and B2C segments. Built an elasticity model mapping price points to estimated demand volume — revealing that B2C mission-aligned buyers showed ~40% WTP premium over comparable non-sustainable furniture.
04
Three-Tier Customer Segmentation
Developed a segmentation framework: Mission-Aligned B2B (restaurants, universities — volume buyers), Design-Forward B2C (urban professionals — premium buyers), and Gift/Occasion (high-margin, low-volume). Each received distinct pricing, messaging, and channel strategy.
05
Executive Presentation Delivery
Synthesized all findings into a 30-slide deck with market sizing, pricing recommendations, channel strategy, and a 12-month implementation roadmap — presented live to ChopValue Boston's leadership team.
04 — Key Findings

Counterintuitive
Strategic Insight

The most counterintuitive finding: B2B clients were more price-sensitive than B2C buyers in the mission-aligned segment. Restaurants evaluated chopstick furniture on cost-per-unit, durability, and lead time — the same criteria as conventional office furniture. Urban professionals buying for home offices or as gifts showed 35–40% willingness to pay above comparable non-sustainable furniture if the provenance story was clearly communicated.

This reversed the initial hypothesis that B2B volume would be the growth engine. The recommendation pivoted toward D2C brand building with B2B serving as a visibility channel — restaurants display ChopValue tables, but the primary revenue comes from D2C buyers who see those tables and look up the brand.

05 — Business Relevance

Why This Matters
in Modern Business

Circular Economy
$4.5T Market by 2030
The Ellen MacArthur Foundation estimates the circular economy will generate $4.5 trillion in economic opportunity by 2030. Pricing strategy for circular products is one of the most critical unsolved problems in sustainable business.
Strategy
Premium Positioning for ESG Products
As ESG metrics enter corporate procurement decisions, B2B buyers are increasingly mandated to source sustainable suppliers. The B2B channel strategy developed here applies to any sustainability-positioned supplier entering corporate supply chains.
Marketing
Provenance as Brand Equity
The finding that "story-telling drives premium pricing" has broad implications — from farm-to-table restaurants to direct-trade coffee. Traceability infrastructure that makes supply chain stories auditable and shareable is a direct business investment.
Finance
Impact Investing Metrics
ChopValue's model provides measurable environmental impact (chopsticks diverted, CO₂ avoided) that can be certified and reported to impact investors — a business model increasingly attractive to ESG-focused venture capital.
06 — Challenges Faced

Real Obstacles
Encountered

Challenge 1
Limited Market Data for a Niche Product
No existing market research covers "upcycled chopstick furniture." Comparable data had to be constructed from adjacent markets (sustainable furniture, B Corp consumer goods) — requiring careful analogical reasoning rather than direct benchmarking.
Challenge 2
Translating Mission into Price
WTP for sustainability is notoriously difficult to measure — consumers over-report it in surveys and under-pay in practice. The conjoint methodology was specifically chosen to reduce social desirability bias by forcing explicit trade-offs between price and product attributes.
Challenge 3
Balancing Mission and Margin
Every pricing recommendation had a tension with ChopValue's mission — higher prices improve margin but limit access, while lower prices broaden reach but threaten the financial sustainability of the circular model. There is no objectively "right" answer; it required explicit value trade-offs.
Challenge 4
Communicating to a Non-Technical Client
Elasticity curves and conjoint utility scores are not intuitive to operators. The 30-slide deck had to translate quantitative analysis into visual narratives and concrete action items that non-analytical stakeholders could adopt immediately.
07 — How It Can Be Improved

Future
Enhancements

1
A/B Price Testing in Production
Replace survey-based WTP estimation with live A/B price testing on the e-commerce channel — actual purchase behavior under different price points is far more reliable than stated preferences.
2
Blockchain Provenance Tracking
Implement QR-code-linked blockchain records showing each product's chopstick count, restaurant of origin, and CO₂ diversion metric. This makes the provenance story verifiable and shareable — directly supporting the premium D2C positioning.
3
Corporate B2B Procurement Targeting
Identify corporations with published circular economy procurement commitments and develop a direct enterprise sales channel — moving beyond restaurant walk-ins to structured B2B deals with multi-year furniture refresh cycles.
