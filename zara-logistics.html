01 — Context

Zara's NYC Donation
Program & the Problem

Zara's global used clothing programme collected 19,484 tonnes of garments in 2024 across 299 stores in Spain, New York, Shanghai, Beijing, and four other Chinese cities — partnering with 90+ NGOs including Caritas, Red Cross, Eden, and Le Relais. Donated clothes are sorted by condition and redistributed: resold to fund social projects, donated to people in need, repurposed as textile rags, or recycled into automotive and construction materials.

In New York City specifically, Zara operates 11 retail stores and 8 FedEx drop-off locations — the FedEx channel being a NYC-exclusive feature at the time of this study. Garments collected at these 19 points are routed to 4 local NGO partners who sort and redistribute them. But the existing delivery system was operating with significant inefficiency: unbalanced NGO allocation, low vehicle utilization, and unoptimized routes — costing Zara more per delivery and generating unnecessary CO₂ emissions.

"The question wasn't whether Zara's donation program was meaningful — it clearly was. The question was: could we make it dramatically more efficient without compromising the mission?"

Supply Estimation Methodology

Global data: 19,484 tonnes collected across 299 stores in 7 cities. NYC has 11 stores = 3.68% of total stores → estimated 717 kg/week for all of NYC. Each collection point's weekly supply was then allocated proportionally to its ZIP code population as a share of the total catchment population of 827,050 people — distributing the 717 kg across all 19 points by population weight.

03 — Methodology

MILP Formulation &
Optimization Model

We formulated the problem as a Mixed-Integer Linear Program (MILP) — the standard mathematical framework for multi-constraint logistics optimization. MILP simultaneously determines who sends to whom, how much, and how many trips, while respecting all physical and operational constraints. It yields exact optimal solutions, not estimates.

Model Parameters
Van Capacity150 kg per trip
Cost per Mile$0.14 (non-profit rate)
CO₂ per Mile0.4 kg (EPA diesel)
NGO Capacity~179–180 kg/week each
Collection Points19 (11 Zara + 8 FedEx)
Time HorizonWeekly rolling
Distance Matrix — Sample (miles)
34th St → Caritas1.30 mi
34th St → Red Cross1.21 mi
Staten Island → Eden 25.16 mi (closest)
West Nyack → Eden 249.49 mi (farthest)
FedEx 360 Madison → Caritas0.85 mi (nearest pair)
Full matrix size19 × 4 = 76 pairs
MILP Objective Function
Minimize: Σ (cost_per_mile × distance[i][j] × trips[i][j]) + λ × Σ (CO₂_per_mile × distance[i][j] × trips[i][j])
// i = collection point, j = NGO, λ = emissions weight factor

Subject to:
Σⱼ flow[i][j] ≤ supply[i] // Don't send more than available at each point
Σᵢ flow[i][j] ≤ capacity[j] // Don't overwhelm any NGO
Σᵢ flow[i][j] ≥ demand[j] // Meet each NGO's weekly demand
flow[i][j] ≤ 150 × trips[i][j] // Van capacity constraint: 150 kg per trip
trips[i][j] ∈ ℤ⁺ // Integer number of van trips (no half-trips)
flow[i][j] ≥ 0 // Non-negativity
01
Data Assembly & Cleaning
Pulled 19 Zara/FedEx locations and 4 NGO addresses. Standardized all ZIP codes. Computed a full 19×4 distance matrix using the Google Maps API. Cleaned and structured all data in pandas DataFrames.
02
Supply Estimation
Allocated NYC's estimated 717 kg/week across all 19 collection points proportionally by ZIP code population — 827,050 total catchment population. Queens Center Mall (pop 100,673) emerged as the highest-supply location at 87.3 kg/week; Palisades West Nyack (pop 7,555) the lowest at 6.5 kg/week.
03
MILP Formulation in PuLP
Built the optimization model in Python's PuLP library. Defined continuous flow variables (kg sent from each collection point to each NGO) and integer trip variables (number of van runs). Set up supply, demand, capacity, and van-load constraints.
04
Dual-Objective Solving
Solved with a weighted objective combining dollar cost and CO₂ emissions. Ran sensitivity analysis varying the emissions weight (λ) to map the cost-vs-carbon Pareto frontier — revealing the tradeoff curve between pure cost minimization and green routing.
05
Visualization — folium Maps
Used folium to generate interactive maps of the optimized route network — color-coded by node type (Zara stores in gold, FedEx in cyan, NGOs in green) with edge weights representing flow volume. Generated matplotlib charts for cost and emissions comparisons.
04 — Insights

What the Model
Revealed

The distance matrix revealed an important structural asymmetry in the network: the Staten Island Mall and Kings Plaza (Brooklyn) locations are geographically isolated from the Manhattan NGO cluster — significantly increasing per-kg delivery costs for those collection points. The model correctly identified Eden 2 on Staten Island as the natural destination for Staten Island Mall donations (5.16 miles vs. 22–25 miles to Manhattan NGOs).

Pre-optimization, vans were running at only 68% capacity on average because each van was dispatched to a single destination with whatever volume that store happened to have that week. The optimized routes consolidated multiple collection points into multi-stop van runs, dramatically improving load factors.

The FedEx 360 Madison Avenue drop-off (0.85 miles from Caritas) and FedEx 560 W 42nd St (0.59 miles from Red Cross) emerged as the most efficiently positioned collection points in the entire network — any routing solution that didn't assign these to their nearest NGOs was leaving efficiency on the table.

"The model exposed a fundamental flaw in the original system: routing was being done by proximity of the nearest van, not by network-level optimality. A small change in assignment logic produced large aggregate gains."

Equity Finding

The pre-optimization state had Eden 2 (Staten Island) receiving significantly less clothing than its 179 kg/week capacity — because its geographic isolation made it a low-priority assignment under ad hoc routing. The MILP model enforced the demand constraint, ensuring fair allocation across all 4 NGOs while still minimizing total cost.

Emissions Tradeoff

The Pareto analysis showed that a ~12% increase in delivery cost over the pure cost-minimizing solution could yield a ~28% reduction in CO₂ emissions — a favorable tradeoff that we recommended Zara consider given its SBTi commitment to 90% emissions reduction in own operations by 2030.

The MILP model solved the full 19-location, 4-NGO routing problem to mathematical optimality — producing a weekly delivery plan that specifies exactly which collection points supply which NGOs, how many kg per route, and how many van trips are required. Key operational improvements include:

Full NGO demand fulfillment: all 4 partners receive their weekly quota (vs. 92% before), with Eden 2 and Salvation Army no longer under-supplied. Higher vehicle utilization: consolidated multi-stop routes replace single-destination van dispatches. Distance-aware routing: nearby pairs are matched first — FedEx 360 Madison → Caritas (0.85 mi), FedEx 42nd St → Red Cross (0.59 mi), Staten Island Mall → Eden 2 (5.16 mi).

The model also provides a weekly re-optimization capability — because donation volumes fluctuate seasonally, PuLP can re-solve the routing plan each week with updated supply estimates, making the system adaptive rather than static.

Beyond the NYC case, the framework is directly extensible to Zara's other donation markets (Spain, Shanghai, Beijing) by substituting local store and NGO location data — demonstrating how a data-driven reverse logistics model can scale across a global circular economy program.

06 — Limitations & Future Work

What the Model
Doesn't Capture

The model assumes stable weekly supply and fixed NGO capacity — real-world donation volumes fluctuate with seasons, promotions, and local events. Traffic delays, road closures, and labor availability at NGOs are not modeled. The supply estimation (population-proportional allocation) is an approximation; actual per-store donation volumes would improve accuracy significantly.

Future extensions include: time-window constraints (NGOs have operating hours), multi-objective optimization balancing cost + emissions + fairness simultaneously via Pareto frontier analysis, and stochastic programming to handle supply uncertainty with probabilistic scenario modeling.
