01 — What Is It

ARIMA Time Series
Forecasting Models

A systematic application of ARIMA (AutoRegressive Integrated Moving Average) models to real macroeconomic time series — forecasting foreign exchange rates, inflation dynamics, and capital flow patterns across international markets. The project uses AIC/BIC information criteria for principled model selection and evaluates forecast accuracy across multiple time horizons.

Beyond building models, the project confronts a fundamental question in applied econometrics: what can purely statistical time series models actually tell us about macroeconomic dynamics, and where do they inevitably fail? Understanding the answer makes you a better analyst in any data-driven finance, consulting, or strategy role.

02 — Why We Did It

The Goal: Disciplined
Quantitative Forecasting

Every CFO, treasury manager, supply chain planner, and macroeconomic analyst needs to forecast — FX rates for hedging, inflation for pricing, sales trends for inventory. The skill being developed here is not just running ARIMA models but knowing which model to specify, why, and what the output actually means — and crucially, where it should not be trusted.

The dual challenge: selecting the right model using AIC/BIC information criteria, and interpreting forecasts in the context of economic fundamentals — not just as statistical outputs divorced from the real world that generated them.

"A good forecast is not just a number. It is a structured argument about what you believe, what you are uncertain about, and exactly why the model will fail at the boundaries of its assumptions."

03 — How We Did It

Systematic ARIMA
Pipeline in R

01
Stationarity Testing — ADF & KPSS
Applied Augmented Dickey-Fuller tests for unit roots and KPSS tests for stationarity. Differenced non-stationary series appropriately — first differences for I(1) processes — and log-transformed where variance needed stabilizing.
02
Model Identification — ACF/PACF Analysis
Analyzed autocorrelation (ACF) and partial autocorrelation (PACF) functions to identify candidate AR(p) and MA(q) orders. Constructed a grid of ARIMA(p,d,q) specifications for systematic competitive evaluation.
03
Model Selection — AIC vs. BIC
Evaluated all candidate models using both Akaike (AIC) and Bayesian (BIC) information criteria. AIC favors forecast fit; BIC penalizes complexity more strongly — compared both to understand the parsimony-accuracy tradeoff.
04
Diagnostic Checking — Ljung-Box Test
Verified model residuals for white noise using Ljung-Box tests at multiple lags. Checked for heteroscedasticity (ARCH effects) and normality of residuals. Re-specified models where diagnostics failed.
05
Forecast Generation with Uncertainty Bands
Generated point forecasts with 80% and 95% prediction intervals. Contextualized forecast paths against monetary policy expectations, inflation dynamics, and capital flow fundamentals — not treating outputs as standalone statistical artifacts.
04 — Key Findings

What the Models
Revealed

AIC and BIC frequently disagreed. AIC preferred higher-order models with more lags capturing nuanced autocorrelation patterns; BIC selected more parsimonious specifications. Out-of-sample validation showed AIC-selected models modestly outperformed at short horizons (1–3 months), while BIC models were more reliable at 6–12 month horizons — a finding with direct implications for which criterion to use depending on the planning horizon.

FX forecasting proved the most humbling exercise: ARIMA models captured momentum well but systematically missed regime changes driven by central bank policy shifts. This is not a model failure — it is a fundamental limitation of extrapolating historical patterns when the data-generating process can discontinuously change. Recognizing where models fail is at least as important as knowing how to build them.

05 — Business Relevance

Why This Matters
in Modern Business

Treasury
FX Risk Management
Multinationals with revenue in multiple currencies use ARIMA-family models for short-term FX forecasting to determine optimal hedging ratios. A 1% improvement in forecast accuracy on a $1B FX exposure generates $10M in hedging savings.
Supply Chain
Demand Forecasting
The same ARIMA framework applied to sales time series powers inventory optimization systems at Amazon, Walmart, and every major retailer. Forecast accuracy directly determines how much working capital is tied up in inventory buffers.
Finance
Macro Trading Signals
Systematic macro hedge funds use time series models as one input to trading signals on rates, FX, and commodities. Understanding their assumptions and failure modes is essential for knowing when to trust and when to override them.
Consulting
Market Entry Timing
Consultants use macroeconomic forecasts to advise clients on market entry timing, capital deployment, and operational hedging. The ability to construct and critique a forecast model is a core analytical skill in top-tier strategy consulting.
06 — Challenges Faced

Real Obstacles
Encountered

Challenge 1
Structural Breaks in FX Data
Central bank interventions, COVID-19 shocks, and geopolitical events create structural breaks in FX series — points where the historical statistical relationships no longer hold. ARIMA has no mechanism to detect or model these discontinuities, requiring hybrid approaches.
Challenge 2
AIC vs. BIC Model Disagreement
When AIC and BIC select different models, there is no objective tiebreaker — the right choice depends on the use case (short vs. long horizon), the cost of over-fitting vs. under-fitting, and domain knowledge. This ambiguity is a feature of honest model selection, not a bug.
Challenge 3
Prediction Interval Communication
Stakeholders consistently interpret point forecasts as certainties and ignore confidence intervals. Communicating that "our 95% interval for the EUR/USD rate in 6 months spans $0.12" — a genuinely uncertain and useful finding — without losing the audience requires careful visualization design.
Challenge 4
Model Validation on Short Series
Macroeconomic data with monthly frequency gives only 10–15 out-of-sample observations for a 1–2 year holdout period — statistically insufficient for robust model selection. This is an inherent limitation of macroeconomic forecasting that cannot be engineered away.
07 — How It Can Be Improved

Future
Enhancements

1
GARCH for Volatility Modeling
Add GARCH (Generalized Autoregressive Conditional Heteroscedasticity) models to explicitly forecast FX volatility — critical for options pricing and dynamic hedging strategies where variance, not just the mean, matters.
2
VAR for Multivariate Dynamics
Replace univariate ARIMA with Vector Autoregression (VAR) to model interactions between FX rates, inflation, and capital flows simultaneously — capturing cross-series feedback that univariate models inherently miss.
3
Bayesian Model Averaging
Instead of selecting a single best model, compute a weighted average of forecasts across the model space weighted by posterior model probabilities — reducing the model uncertainty that AIC/BIC selection silently discards.
