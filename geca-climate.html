01 — Context

Why Model
Climate as a System?

Climate change is the canonical complex systems problem — it exhibits nonlinear feedbacks, long time delays, deep uncertainty, and civilization-scale stakes. Traditional linear analysis fails to capture why the climate system can appear stable for decades and then tip rapidly. System dynamics modeling — the discipline of understanding behavior through feedback structures and stock accumulation — exists precisely for this class of problem.

The project, formally titled "Human Activity → Emissions → Global Warming: Narrative, Data, Model & Critique", modeled the full causal chain from human economic activity through GHG emissions to atmospheric accumulation, temperature rise, and cascading climate impacts. The goal was not just to simulate — but to identify where and how interventions can break reinforcing loops and strengthen balancing mechanisms before tipping points make change structurally difficult.

The system's danger lies not in any single emission event but in stock accumulation: natural sinks (land + ocean) absorb roughly half of annual CO₂, leaving the rest to accumulate permanently in the atmosphere. Unlike a flow problem (reduce the rate, fix the problem), a stock problem requires sustained intervention over decades before concentrations stabilize — a counterintuitive reality that most policy timelines underestimate.

"In a stock-and-flow system, cutting emissions by 50% does not cut the atmospheric stock by 50% — it merely slows the rate of accumulation. This distinction is the most important thing most policymakers don't understand."

02 — System Structure

Stocks, Flows &
Feedback Architecture

The Vensim model was built around a core stock-and-flow structure with four key stocks: Atmospheric CO₂, Global Temperature, Ice Coverage, and Permafrost Mass. Human activity sectors (agriculture, industry, transport, energy) drive emission flows into the atmospheric stock. Natural absorption (land + ocean sinks) partially counters the accumulation, but at a declining rate as sinks saturate.

Core Stock-Flow Equations
d[CO₂]/dt = Emissions(t) − NaturalAbsorption(t)
// Natural sinks (land + ocean) remove ~50% of annual CO₂; the rest accumulates permanently

ΔF = 5.35 · ln(C / C₀) // Radiative forcing (W/m²), C₀=278 ppm preindustrial baseline — IPCC Myhre et al.

ΔT = λ · ΔF // Temperature anomaly, λ = climate sensitivity 0.6–1.0 °C per W/m²

I(t) ∝ ΔT² // Impact intensity increases nonlinearly — amplified by reinforcing feedback loops

Baseline Parameters (2025)

AF (airborne fraction) = 0.40–0.55 · 1 ppm ≈ 2.12 GtC ≈ 7.8 GtCO₂
Feedback Loop Architecture
R1 — Reinforcing Ice-Albedo Feedback
As temperature rises, Arctic and Antarctic ice melts. Ice reflects 80–90% of incoming solar radiation (high albedo); open ocean absorbs 94%. As ice coverage drops, the planet absorbs more solar energy, warming further — which melts more ice. This loop is self-accelerating once triggered and represents one of the most dangerous tipping elements in the climate system.
↑ Temperature → ↓ Ice Cover → ↓ Albedo → ↑ Solar Absorption → ↑ Temperature (R+)
R2 — Reinforcing Permafrost Methane Loop
As global temperatures rise, permafrost (frozen ground in Arctic regions containing millennia of organic matter) begins to thaw. Thawing permafrost releases CH₄ (methane) — a GHG ~80x more potent than CO₂ over 20 years. This CH₄ raises atmospheric GHG concentrations, accelerating warming, which triggers more thaw. The estimated carbon stock in permafrost (~1,700 GtC) exceeds total human industrial emissions since 1750.
↑ Temperature → ↑ Permafrost Thaw → ↑ CH₄ Release → ↑ GHG Stock → ↑ Temperature (R+)
B1 — Balancing Natural Carbon Sinks
Land ecosystems (forests, soils) and oceans act as carbon sinks — absorbing approximately 50% of annual anthropogenic CO₂ emissions. As atmospheric CO₂ rises, some additional CO₂ fertilization of vegetation occurs, slightly increasing sink capacity. However, warming and deforestation are degrading sinks over time — threatening to flip this balancing loop into a neutral or even reinforcing dynamic.
↑ CO₂ Stock → ↑ Absorption Rate → ↓ Net CO₂ Accumulation (B−)
B2 — Balancing Policy & Technology Response
As climate impacts become more severe and observable, political pressure and economic incentives for clean technology adoption grow. Renewables displace fossil fuels, methane leak regulations reduce CH₄ emissions, and monitoring systems provide faster feedback to decision-makers — compressing the information delay that historically slows climate policy response. This is the primary human-controllable balancing loop in the system.
↑ Climate Impacts → ↑ Policy Pressure → ↑ Clean Tech Adoption → ↓ Emissions → ↓ CO₂ Stock (B−)
Critical System Insight — Information Delay

The model revealed that information delays are structurally central to climate system failure. There is a ~15–20 year lag between emission decisions and observable temperature impacts. By the time the consequences of today's emissions are felt, the atmospheric stock has already been locked in for decades. Faster monitoring (satellite + in-situ data integration into real-time dashboards) compresses this delay — which is why the "Policy + Monitoring" scenario outperforms "Strong Policy" alone even at the same emission reduction rate.

Key Finding — The Value of Monitoring

The gap between Strong Policy and Policy + Monitoring scenarios (~5–10 ppm CO₂, ~0.1°C ΔT by 2050) is entirely driven by information delay compression — not by different emission reduction targets. Faster real-time climate data allows policymakers to detect threshold crossings earlier and activate balancing responses before reinforcing loops gain irreversible momentum. This quantifies the ROI of climate monitoring infrastructure investment.

05 — Actionable Levers

Breaking the Loops —
Workable Interventions

The system model identified where in the causal structure interventions have the highest leverage — targeting the largest emission flows, the most dangerous reinforcing loops, and the most impactful information delays. Real-world implementation examples were drawn from Germany, Denmark, the Netherlands, Canada, and Norway:

Energy
Electricity Decarbonization
Accelerate clean generation to ≥50–60% by 2035 via renewables + storage + transmission. Retire unabated coal; expand demand-side flexibility. Germany's renewable transition avoided ~250–300 million tonnes CO₂/year; Denmark ~25–30 million tonnes/year.
Transport
Zero-Emission Vehicle Transition
Phase in ZEV mandates and charging infrastructure. Shift freight to rail; adopt low-carbon aviation fuels on critical routes. Tighten CH₄ leak detection and repair in oil & gas supply chains (LDAR protocols).
Methane
30% Methane Reduction by 2030
Deliver ≥30% CH₄ reductions from oil & gas, waste, and agriculture via LDAR, anaerobic digestion, and livestock feed additives. Methane's 20-year warming potential is ~80x CO₂ — highest near-term leverage point for slowing the permafrost loop.
Land
End Net Deforestation by 2030
Halt deforestation to preserve balancing loop B1 (natural sinks). Restore forests and soils; implement climate-smart agriculture (precision irrigation, drought-resilient crops). Protects ~500 GtC stored in terrestrial ecosystems from re-entering the emission flow.
CCS
Carbon Capture & Infrastructure
Canada's Boundary Dam: captured 0.85 million tonnes CO₂/year. Norway's Longship Project: capturing 1.5 million tonnes CO₂/year. The Netherlands Delta Programme uses "Dike-ring areas" to maintain flood mortality at 1-in-100,000 per year through 2050 — a model for climate-resilient infrastructure design.
Monitor
Real-Time Monitoring Integration
Integrate satellite + in-situ observational data into real-time dashboards for floods, droughts, and heatwaves. This is the highest-leverage structural intervention because it compresses information delays — accelerating B2 (policy response loop) without requiring additional emission reductions.
06 — Model Critique

What Our Model Captures —
and What It Misses

We critically evaluated our Vensim model against the University of Chicago's interactive climate model suite (MODTRAN, RRTM, GEOCARB, SLUGULATOR, ISAM, GRANTISM, PERMAFROST) — web-based educational tools used widely for conceptual climate scenario exploration.

Shared strengths with Chicago models: transparent assumptions, accessible scenario exploration, adjustable parameters, causal mechanism illustration. These tools are powerful for education and preliminary analysis. Our Vensim model adds stock-flow formalization, explicit feedback loop mapping, and quantitative scenario projections.

Shared limitations: both approaches use simplified dynamics and partial feedback representation compared to full IPCC Earth System Models (ESMs) that integrate ocean-atmosphere-biosphere dynamics. Neither our model nor Chicago's tools ingest real-time observational datasets (NOAA, Copernicus), provide formal uncertainty quantification with confidence intervals, or model tipping point probability distributions.

Appropriate Use

Both our model and the Chicago interactive tools are best used for conceptual insight, stakeholder communication, and preliminary scenario framing — not for policy-grade forecasting or investment-grade climate risk assessment. For those purposes, full ESMs with vetted NOAA/EPA/IPCC datasets and formal sensitivity analysis are required. This honest scoping is itself a systems thinking discipline: knowing your model's validity boundary.

The model's applications extend well beyond the classroom. The framework directly supports disaster preparedness (flood risk mapping for infrastructure and insurance), carbon management (quantifying the ROI of emission reduction policies), agricultural planning (scenario-based water resource management under each temperature trajectory), and policy analysis (centralized real-time data collection as a force multiplier for any emission reduction commitment).

The most practically important finding — that monitoring investment has quantifiable emission-equivalent value — is a systems insight that traditional cost-benefit analyses of climate policy routinely miss, because they model monitoring as an overhead cost rather than a feedback-loop intervention.
